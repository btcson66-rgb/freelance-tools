Charging fairly for urgent, expedited work

When a client needs it faster than normal, they are asking you to disrupt your schedule, your other clients, and possibly your weekend. A rush fee prices that disruption honestly, and presented well, clients accept it as standard.

Freelance Tools · Updated June 2026

Why urgent work genuinely costs more

A rush job is not just the same work sooner; it is work that displaces other commitments, compresses your time, and often means evenings or weekends. That disruption has a real cost, and a rush fee simply prices it. Charging more for urgency is not opportunism — it is the same logic that makes expedited shipping or next-day services cost more everywhere, because speed consumes capacity that would otherwise serve other work.

Decide what actually counts as a rush

A rush fee only makes sense if you define what triggers it. Typically it is a deadline shorter than your normal turnaround, or a request that requires you to reshuffle committed work. Setting a clear threshold — faster than a stated standard lead time — means you are applying a consistent rule rather than charging extra based on mood, which clients sense and resent. Define it once so every rush request meets the same standard.

Set the premium deliberately

Rush premiums are commonly expressed as a percentage uplift on the normal fee, sized to reflect how much disruption the speed demands. A modest deadline compression might warrant a small uplift; dropping everything for an overnight turnaround warrants more. The right number is one that genuinely compensates you for the displaced work and the strain, not a token that leaves you resentful for having said yes to chaos.

Put it in your terms before it is needed

The easiest rush fee to charge is one the client already knew about. Stating your standard turnaround and your rush policy in your agreement or proposal up front means that when an urgent request arrives, the fee is a pre-agreed term rather than a surprise. A client who accepted your rush policy at the outset cannot reasonably object when you apply it, which removes the awkwardness from the conversation entirely.

Present it as a choice, not a penalty

Frame the rush fee as an option the client controls: the standard timeline at the standard price, or the expedited timeline at the expedited price. This positions the fee as a service you are offering rather than a punishment for their urgency. Most clients facing a real deadline are happy to pay for speed, and giving them a clear choice lets them decide based on how much the urgency is worth to them.

Protect the quality and your boundaries

A rush fee should not become a standing invitation to abuse your time. Be willing to say a deadline is not feasible at any price if it would compromise quality or your wellbeing, and reserve the right to decline. The fee compensates for reasonable disruption; it does not obligate you to accept every emergency. Protecting that line keeps rush work occasional and profitable rather than a constant, draining norm.

Use it to reward planning

Over time, a consistent rush policy gently teaches clients that planning ahead is cheaper than emergencies, which improves your working relationship and your schedule. The clients who value your work will start giving you proper lead time to avoid the premium, and the occasional genuine rush is fairly paid. Either outcome is good: you are compensated for urgency or rewarded with a saner calendar.

Be clear with yourself about the difference between a genuine rush and a client who is simply disorganized, because the two deserve different responses. A true rush is occasional and worth pricing; chronic last-minute requests from the same client are a relationship problem a fee alone will not fix. In those cases, a conversation about better lead times serves you more than repeatedly cashing rush premiums for avoidable chaos.

When you do deliver rushed work, make the expedited effort visible so the premium feels earned. A short note that you rearranged other commitments to hit their deadline reminds the client what the fee bought. People pay more willingly when they perceive the extra effort behind the price, and quietly absorbing the disruption while charging for it can leave a client wondering what the premium was even for.
