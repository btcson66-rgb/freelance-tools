What freelancers should review financially in June

June is a useful checkpoint. There is enough of the year behind you to see patterns, and enough time left to change pricing, spending, taxes, and sales habits before year end.

Freelance Tools · Updated June 2026

Use June as a checkpoint, not a judgment day

A mid-year financial review should be practical, not dramatic. By June, you have enough real activity to see what the year is becoming. You can compare revenue against expectations, notice which clients are carrying the business, check whether expenses crept up, and adjust before the second half gets away from you. I like this review because it is early enough to change behavior. A year-end review is useful, but it often tells you what already happened. A June review can still affect the outcome.

Gather the basic numbers first

Start with revenue collected, invoices sent but not paid, expenses paid, taxes set aside, cash in the bank, and committed work for the next few months. Keep the categories clean. Collected revenue and invoiced revenue are not the same. Signed work and likely work are not the same. If your records are messy, do not spend the whole day building a perfect accounting system. Get enough clarity to make decisions, then improve the system later.

A simple offline expense tracker like CashFlow can make this review less painful because the questions are basic: what came in, what went out, what is still owed, and what does that leave? The goal is not to create a beautiful report. The goal is to understand whether the business is healthier, tighter, or more fragile than it felt during the daily work.

Compare revenue to capacity

Look at how much you earned in the first half of the year and compare it to how busy you were. High revenue with unsustainable hours is not as good as it looks. Lower revenue with a light calendar may signal a sales problem or an intentional season. This is where freelance numbers need context. A simple total does not tell you whether your pricing is working, whether your calendar is overloaded, or whether a few clients are hiding weak demand elsewhere.

Check your break-even point

Run your current monthly costs through the freelance break-even calculator. Do not use the lean number from January if your spending changed. Include business essentials, personal draws, recurring software, insurance, taxes, and debt obligations. The break-even number is the line between stability and drift. If the number rose without a matching increase in revenue, you need to know that now, not after several slow months.

Review expenses by usefulness

Expense review should not become a guilt exercise. The question is whether each cost still earns its place. Some tools save time, reduce mistakes, or help you get paid. Others were useful during a past phase and now sit there quietly billing you. Sort expenses into keep, downgrade, pause, and cancel. Be careful with costs that support delivery or sales. Cutting a tool that saves hours every month may be false economy. Cutting a forgotten subscription is just maintenance.

Check tax set-asides before they become urgent

June is a good time to ask whether your tax savings match your actual year so far. If income rose, you may be under-saving. If income fell, you may have more cushion than expected, though I would still be careful. Use the freelance tax estimator to sanity-check the direction. It is not a replacement for an accountant, but it can show whether your set-aside habit needs attention before the end of the year.

Look for client concentration

Freelancers often feel stable because work is busy, but the stability may depend on one client. In your mid-year review, calculate how much of your revenue came from each client. If one client dominates the year, that is not automatically bad. It does mean you should treat sales pipeline seriously even while busy. A large client can be great, but it should not become a reason to ignore business development until the moment the client pauses spending.

Review pricing with evidence

Look at completed projects and ask what they really paid after meetings, revisions, admin, and delays. If you track time, compare estimated effort with actual effort. If you do not, use calendar notes and memory carefully. The pattern matters more than one odd project. Are certain services always heavier than expected? Do certain clients require more management? Are rush projects priced high enough to compensate for disruption? Mid-year is a good time to adjust rates or scope language before quoting the next round of work.

Clean up receivables

List every unpaid invoice and sort by age. Recent invoices may only need normal follow-up. Older invoices need direct action. Send reminders, confirm the right accounts payable contact, and decide when to pause work for accounts that are overdue. Unpaid invoices distort your sense of how the business is doing. Revenue is not very useful if it sits in someone else's payment queue for months. Treat receivables cleanup as part of the review, not a separate chore.

Set second-half targets you can act on

Finish the review with a short second-half plan. Pick a revenue target, a minimum monthly cash balance, a tax set-aside habit, a sales activity rhythm, and one pricing or scope change. Keep it small enough to use. A financial review that produces twenty goals usually produces no change. A review that says raise new project minimums, follow up weekly on receivables, and keep two months of expenses in cash is much more likely to affect the business.

FAQ

Why should freelancers do a mid-year financial review?
June gives enough data to see real patterns while still leaving time to adjust pricing, expenses, taxes, and sales activity before year end.
What numbers should I gather first?
Collect revenue received, unpaid invoices, expenses, tax savings, bank balance, and committed upcoming work. Keep actual cash separate from expected income.
Should I cut expenses during the review?
Cut or pause expenses that no longer help. Keep costs that clearly support delivery, sales, accuracy, or getting paid.
How do I know whether my pricing needs to change?
Compare completed projects against real effort, meetings, revisions, admin, and stress. Repeated underestimation is evidence that pricing or scope needs adjustment.
What should come out of the review?
A short second-half plan with a revenue target, cash minimum, tax habit, receivables follow-up routine, and one or two pricing changes.
