How to get your freelance books ready for your accountant

Accountant prep is mostly about reducing mystery. Clean income, categorized expenses, saved receipts, tax notes, and clear questions make the meeting faster and more useful.

Freelance Tools · Updated June 2026

Start earlier than the deadline

Getting books ready for an accountant is harder when it becomes a deadline scramble. The work is not complicated, but it does require attention. You need income records, expense categories, bank statements, receipts, invoices, tax payments, and notes about anything unusual. If you wait until the accountant asks, every missing receipt feels urgent. I prefer to do a cleanup pass before the formal deadline so there is time to find documents and ask better questions.

Separate business and personal activity

If you already have separate business accounts, this step is easier. If not, go through the year and mark which transactions were truly business related. Do not guess casually. A clean separation helps your accountant work faster and reduces the chance that personal spending gets treated as a business expense. It also helps you see the actual economics of the freelance business. Mixed accounts make everything feel foggier than it needs to be.

Reconcile income first

Make a list of invoices issued, payments received, refunds, chargebacks, platform payouts, and any income that did not go through your usual invoice process. Then compare that list to bank deposits and payment processor records. The goal is to explain every income line. If a deposit combines several client payments, note that. If a client paid late in the next year, note that too. Income confusion can create bigger problems than a missing small receipt.

Categorize expenses in plain language

Use categories your accountant can understand: software, contractor payments, advertising, office supplies, education, travel, meals, equipment, professional services, and payment processing fees. Do not create clever categories that only make sense to you. If something is ambiguous, add a note instead of forcing it. For example, a coworking charge may be office expense, but a client lunch may need more detail. Good notes save back-and-forth later.

Collect receipts and proof

Bank statements show that money moved. Receipts explain what it was for. Save receipts for business purchases, especially larger items, travel, meals, equipment, and anything that might not be obvious from the merchant name. If a receipt is missing, write a note with the date, amount, vendor, purpose, and how it supported the business. Your accountant can tell you what is acceptable, but a contemporaneous note is better than silence.

Review contractor and subcontractor payments

If you paid other freelancers or subcontractors, gather names, amounts, payment dates, invoices, and tax forms if applicable. Make sure the expense is tied to the project or business purpose. Contractor payments are easy to forget if they happened through bank transfer, payment apps, or platform payouts. Your accountant needs the full picture, not just the expenses that went through a credit card.

Estimate taxes before the meeting

You do not need to calculate the final tax bill yourself, but you should have a rough idea of where you stand. Use the freelance tax estimator to sanity-check income, expenses, and set-asides. This helps you ask better questions: am I saving enough, should I adjust estimated payments, and did my profit change enough to affect planning? The goal is not to replace the accountant. It is to arrive informed.

Check sales tax or VAT questions

Sales tax and VAT rules depend on location, service type, client location, thresholds, and other details. Do not assume you are exempt just because you are a freelancer, and do not assume you owe it on everything. Gather where your clients are, what you sold, and any tax collected. The sales tax VAT calculator can help you think through amounts, but your accountant should confirm the rule that applies to your situation.

List unusual events

Accountants need context for anything outside the normal pattern. Did you buy a laptop, move states, start selling digital products, hire contractors, receive a grant, refund a large client payment, write off bad debt, or switch business entities? Make a simple list. You may not know which events matter, and that is fine. The accountant can decide. Your job is to surface the events so they are not discovered later by accident.

Prepare questions instead of vague worry

Before the meeting, write the questions you actually need answered. Examples: Should I change my estimated tax payment? Can I deduct part of my home office? Should I form an entity? How should I handle payments from clients in other countries? What records should I keep better next year? Specific questions make the meeting more valuable. Vague worry tends to produce vague reassurance.

Make next year easier

After the accountant finishes, ask what would make next year's prep cleaner. Maybe they want categories changed, receipts stored differently, tax payments recorded in a specific way, or sales tax separated more clearly. Turn that advice into a simple monthly routine. Accountant prep should get easier every year. If it does not, the system is too dependent on memory and last-minute energy.

FAQ

What should I give my accountant as a freelancer?
Provide income records, invoices, expense categories, receipts, bank statements, tax payments, contractor payments, and notes about unusual events.
Do bank statements replace receipts?
No. Bank statements show payment, while receipts explain what was purchased and why it was business related.
Should I estimate taxes before talking to my accountant?
A rough estimate helps you ask better questions, but your accountant should confirm the final treatment and payment plan.
What if my business and personal expenses are mixed?
Mark business transactions carefully and add notes where needed. Then consider separating accounts going forward to make records cleaner.
How can I make accountant prep easier next year?
Ask your accountant what records they want, then create a monthly routine for categorizing expenses, saving receipts, and recording tax payments.
