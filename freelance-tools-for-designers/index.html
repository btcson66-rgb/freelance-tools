Why designers keep getting scope creep

Most scope creep in design projects comes from one missing document: an out-of-scope list in the contract. If the agreement says "website design" without specifying what that means, the client has room to ask for extra pages, redesigns, additional breakpoints, illustration, and "just one more round of revisions." A signed contract with a clear scope list, a revision count and a change-request process turns those requests from arguments into billed work. What the design contract must say about IP

The IP clause is where most design contracts fail. Without it, copyright law in many countries means the designer retains ownership of the work — which is often not what either party expects. State clearly: the final deliverables transfer to the client on full payment; all drafts, concepts, source files and pre-existing tools and templates remain yours; third-party assets (stock, fonts, plugins) remain under their original licenses. The ProposalPro clause library covers this in plain English.

What should a design contract include?
Scope (deliverables, file formats, revisions), payment terms and deposit, IP ownership (transfers on full payment), revision and change-request process, portfolio rights, and kill fee.
How do designers invoice clients?
Include invoice number, date, due date, your and client's details, line-by-line work breakdown, tax, and total. An invoice generator handles the formatting and math.
